Since the number of planets is odd, we can consider the number of planets as (2n + 1) where n is a positive integer. Now, we consider a binary tree (a tree whose number of children per node is at least two) where each node has only one child. If each node represents a planet then each edge represents the line of sight between two particular planets. Now, for each planet to be watched, the total number of edges for the binary tree with (2n + 1) nodes should be (2n + 1). Thanks to Glenn for these three points:

Note 1: a straight edge does *not* contain a scale. Measuring distances by using a scale is not an allowed operation in classical compass/straightedge constructions.

A town has two hospitals, one big and one small. Every day the big hospital delivers 1000 babies and the small hospital delivers 100 babies. There's a 50/50 chance of male or female on each birth. Which hospital has a better chance of having the same number of boys as girls? You must explain your answer.

Answer

The hospital with fewer babies has a higher probability.

A Bernoulli trial is an experiment having only two possible outcomes: success or failure. Flipping a coin is an example of a Bernoulli trial. Whether it snows on Christmas Day or not is a Bernoulli trial. Whether you get called on to recite in Spanish class is a Bernoulli trial. (You decide which alternative is a success and which is a failure.) Similarly, whether a male is born or not (and hence a female is born) is also a Bernoulli trial.

Theorem: Suppose a Bernoulli trial has probability p of success and probability q = 1 - p of failure. Then the probability of obtaining exactly k successes in n independent trials is
P(k) = C(n, k) ·p^k·q^ (n-k).

For the hospital with a 100 babies, n=100, k=50, p=0.5, q=0.5. Therefore, P(50) = 0.07959

Similarly, for the hospital with a 1000 babies, n=1000, k=500, p=0.5, q=0.5. Therefore, P(500) = 0.02523

We can see that P(50) > P(500). Hence the hospital with a 100 babies has a higher chance.

Answer

The car travelling westwards will wear its tires off first.

This puzzle deals with physics. For an object that travels uniformly in a circular manner (uniform circular motion), it will experience a centrifugal force radically outwards. This force increases with the speed of the circular motion.

Hence the faster a body travels, the lesser it will weight (weight = gravitational contribution - centrifugal contribution).

The earth rotates from west to east. Hence the car moving eastwards, will be moving faster (relative motion around the centre of the earth) than the car westwards. Since friction is proportional to the normal reaction (weight), the car travelling westwards will wear its tires off first.

For n = 5 and 6, use 1.00 = .50 + .25 + .10 + .10 + .05, then .50 = 2*.25.

For n = 7 to 9, use 1.00 = 2*.25 + 5*.10, then apply .10 = 2*.05 twice.

For n = 10 to 19, use 1.00 = 10*.10 and apply .10 = 2*.05 nine times.

For n = 20 to 76, vary the breakdown of the first 30 cents from 3 to 6 coins using .30 = 3*.10 = 2*.10 + 2*.05 = .10 + 4*.05 = 6*.05; the remaining 70 cents starts out as nickels which you convert to pennies one at a time, raising the count by 4 each time. Thus you get from 20 nickels to 70 pennies and 6 nickels.

To get 77 coins, suppose these include 5k pennies (must have a multiple of 5 because every other coin is congruent to 0 mod 5 cents). Then the remaining 100 - 5k cents must consist of 77 - 5k coins. Since each remaining coin is worth at least 5 cents, the non-penny value in cents is at least 5 times the non-penny coin count:

   100 - 5k  >=  5 * (77 - 5k)

By algebra:

   20 - k  >=  77 - 5k

   4k  >=  57

   k  >=  57/4 = 14.25

Since k is an integer, k >= 15, i.e. we must have at least 5*15 = 75 pennies.  We can't have more because 5*16 = 80 is already greater than 77.  But then we must find exactly two coins to add up to the remaining 25 cents.  By exhaustive search we show there is no such combination.  We can't make 1.00 in 77 coins.

At this time that person has 4 times the money to his right person. How many men are there along with Ramesh and what is the money with poorest fellow?

Answer

7 and 2

Whenever one gets some money and passes appropriate amount to his right, he is depreciated by 1 rupee.
Ramesh has money R initially, his right one has (R-1), and his right one has (R-2) and so on. After first round, money with Ramesh is (R-1); his right one has (R-2), and so on. Actually 1 rs from each is extracted and collected rupees are finally given to the last person on the round table who is the poorest fellow. This person finally gives these rupees to Ramesh to start the next round.

n = number of persons on round table
R = rupees with Ramesh initially
R-n+1 = rupees with poorest fellow initially

After round 1,

Rupees reduced from all = n
Rs with Ramesh = (R-1)
Rs with poorest fellow = (R-n)

After round 2,

Rupees reduced from all = n + n
Rs with Ramesh = (R-2)
Rs with poorest fellow = (R-n-1)

         |

         |

         |

After round k,

Rupees reduced from all = n.k
Rs with Ramesh = (R-k)
Rs with poorest fellow = R-n-(k-1)
After this round, rs with poorest fellow becomes 0.

After round k+1,

Rupees reduced from all = n.k + (n-1)
Rs with Ramesh = (R-k-1)
